# SG90 Servo Convention Research

## Executive summary

The practical consensus for hobby and classroom use is to treat a hobby servo command as a repeated high pulse whose width sets position, with about 1500 µs as neutral and about a 20 ms / 50 Hz refresh period as the safe default ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs), [SparkFun Hobby Servo Tutorial](https://learn.sparkfun.com/tutorials/hobby-servo-tutorial/all), [Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf)).  The conservative RC-compatible command range is normally taught as 1000-2000 µs around a 1500 µs center, while SG90-related datasheets and Arduino defaults commonly expose a wider range such as 500-2400 µs or 544-2400 µs ([Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf), [Arduino Servo library source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arduino-libraries/Servo/master/src/Servo.h), [Arduino Servo.attach reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-attach)).  Direction is not globally standardized: Arduino-style documentation often says 1000 µs is fully counter-clockwise, 1500 µs is middle, and 2000 µs is fully clockwise, but Pololu explicitly warns that the direction for increasing pulse width is not standardized and may be reversed on another servo model ([Arduino Servo.writeMicroseconds reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-writemicroseconds), [Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).

For STEM education up to Grade 12, the clearest convention is to teach two layers: use `Servo.write(angle)` on a 0-180 command scale for programming, and explain that the underlying signal is approximately 1000 µs at 0, 1500 µs at 90, and 2000 µs at 180 for a conservative setup ([Arduino Servo.write reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-write), [Arduino Servo Motor Basics](https://docs.arduino.cc/learn/electronics/servo-motors/), [Adafruit Arduino Lesson 14](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-arduino-lesson-14-servo-motors/servo-motors)).  Direction should be taught only with an explicit viewing reference, such as “viewed from the output shaft/horn side,” because “left,” “right,” “clockwise,” and “counter-clockwise” are otherwise ambiguous for a mounted servo ([MakeCode Adafruit servoWrite](https://makecode.adafruit.com/reference/pins/servo-write), [Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).

## 1. Pulse-width specifications and conventions

### Consensus signal shape and refresh rate

A standard hobby servo is controlled by a pulse train in which pulse width is the command variable and the repetition period is commonly about 20 ms, or 50 Hz ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs), [SparkFun Hobby Servo Tutorial](https://learn.sparkfun.com/tutorials/hobby-servo-tutorial/all), [Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf)).  Pololu describes the command as a square wave characterized by pulse voltage, pulse width, and pulse period, and states that the typical pulse period is around 20 ms, corresponding to 50 Hz ([Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).  The SG90 PDF available through Datasheet4U lists a pulse cycle of “ca. 20 ms,” matching the usual classroom and RC convention ([Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf)).

There is consensus that 1500 µs / 1.5 ms is the neutral or center command for standard hobby servos, though actual mechanical center can vary by servo and installation ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs), [Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail), [Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf)).  ServoCity states that every servo has a neutral position “always around 1.5 milliseconds,” while Hitec states that its servo pulse duration is 900-2100 µs with 1500 µs as center and 50 Hz refresh ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs), [Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf)).  Pololu adds the important caveat that the 1.5 ms neutral point is not necessarily the exact middle of a servo’s absolute mechanical range, even among units of the same model ([Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).

### Standard RC range versus SG90 and Arduino ranges

The conservative RC-style convention is roughly 1000 µs to 2000 µs around 1500 µs, and many beginner resources present this as the standard command range ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs), [SparkFun Hobby Servo Tutorial](https://learn.sparkfun.com/tutorials/hobby-servo-tutorial/all), [Adafruit Arduino Lesson 14](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-arduino-lesson-14-servo-motors/servo-motors)).  ServoCity says the minimum valid pulse is generally about 1 ms and the maximum is generally about 2 ms, while SparkFun says pulses occur every 20 ms and vary between 1 ms and 2 ms in width ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs), [SparkFun Hobby Servo Tutorial](https://learn.sparkfun.com/tutorials/hobby-servo-tutorial/all)).  Adafruit’s beginner lesson teaches the same practical mapping as 1 ms for one end, 1.5 ms for center, and 2 ms for the other end, while noting that endpoints vary and many servos only turn about 170 degrees ([Adafruit Arduino Lesson 14](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-arduino-lesson-14-servo-motors/servo-motors)).

The SG90 material is broader and somewhat inconsistent because the Datasheet4U PDF aggregates multiple SG90-related specification blocks, but it clearly lists 180 degrees of rotation, a pulse cycle of about 20 ms, a pulse width of 500-2400 µs, and a dead band width of 10 µs ([Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf)).  The same SG90 PDF also includes a position statement that 1.5 ms is middle and that about 2 ms and about 1 ms correspond to opposite ends, which aligns with the RC convention even though the extracted text around “left/right” is garbled in places ([Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf)).  Arduino hobbyists commonly notice this discrepancy: one Arduino Forum user measured an SG90 moving through about 180 degrees under the Servo library’s wider default range and then found the SG90 page specifying 500-2400 µs, while noting that many references said 1000-2000 µs ([Arduino Forum: Servo pulse widths](https://forum.arduino.cc/t/servo-pulse-widths/286525)).

The Arduino Servo library itself defaults to 544 µs minimum, 2400 µs maximum, 1500 µs default pulse width, and 20,000 µs refresh interval in `Servo.h` ([Arduino Servo library source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arduino-libraries/Servo/master/src/Servo.h)).  The library’s `write()` implementation maps angle values 0-180 linearly onto the attached servo’s configured minimum and maximum pulse widths, and `writeMicroseconds()` clips requested pulse widths to that configured range ([Arduino Servo AVR implementation](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arduino-libraries/Servo/master/src/avr/Servo.cpp)).  The Arduino reference mirror describes `attach(pin, min, max)` as setting the pulse widths corresponding to 0 degrees and 180 degrees, with defaults of 544 and 2400 µs ([Arduino Servo.attach reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-attach)).

### Pulse width versus angle and linearity

Software libraries usually treat the pulse-to-angle relationship as a linear interpolation between configured endpoints, as shown by Arduino’s `map(value, 0, 180, SERVO_MIN(), SERVO_MAX())` implementation ([Arduino Servo AVR implementation](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arduino-libraries/Servo/master/src/avr/Servo.cpp)).  In practice, Pololu warns that there is no standard correspondence between pulse width and absolute shaft position, and that 1.0-2.0 ms may yield different angular travel on different servos ([Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).  SparkFun likewise warns students to be careful with shorter or longer pulses and uses `attach(9, 1000, 2000)` to constrain a classroom example to the 1-2 ms range ([SparkFun Hobby Servo Tutorial](https://learn.sparkfun.com/tutorials/hobby-servo-tutorial/all)).

Dead band is usually specified in microseconds and varies by servo model or programmable setting ([Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf), [Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf), [Hitec HS-5645MG spec sheet](https://www.hiteccs.com/public/uploads/data_sheet/HCS_HS-5645MG_Specsheetv2.2_-1729886570.pdf)).  The SG90 PDF lists a dead band width of 10 µs, Hitec’s programmer manual says Hitec digital servo dead band width can be programmed from about 3 µs to 48 µs, and the Hitec HS-5645MG spec sheet lists a 1 µs deadband ([Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf), [Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf), [Hitec HS-5645MG spec sheet](https://www.hiteccs.com/public/uploads/data_sheet/HCS_HS-5645MG_Specsheetv2.2_-1729886570.pdf)).  For curriculum purposes, the practical implication is that very small command changes near a few microseconds may not produce visible motion, and exact endpoint/angle calibration should be done experimentally for a physical build ([Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf), [Arduino Forum: SG90 PWM settings](https://forum.arduino.cc/t/sg90-servo-pwm-settings/549644)).

## 2. Directionality conventions

The common Arduino-style software convention is that a 1000 µs command is fully counter-clockwise, 1500 µs is middle, and 2000 µs is fully clockwise on a “standard servo” ([Arduino Servo.writeMicroseconds reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-writemicroseconds)).  ServoCity states the same relationship in relative terms: a pulse less than 1.5 ms moves the output shaft counterclockwise from neutral, and a pulse wider than 1.5 ms does the opposite ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs)).  Adafruit MakeCode uses a classroom-friendly wording in which 0 degrees is left / counter-clockwise, 90 degrees is neutral, and 180 degrees is right / clockwise ([MakeCode Adafruit servoWrite](https://makecode.adafruit.com/reference/pins/servo-write)).

There is no reliable industry-wide rule that increasing pulse width always produces clockwise motion on every hobby servo, and Pololu explicitly states that “the direction that a servo turns for increasing pulse widths is not standardized” ([Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).  Hitec’s programmer manual also treats direction as a servo/programmer parameter because it includes a rotation setting that can select clockwise or counter-clockwise on programmable Hitec digital servos ([Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf)).  Arduino Forum discussions show the same real-world problem: one user quoted Arduino’s 1000-CCW / 2000-CW wording but reported TowerPro servos that moved the opposite way when pulse width increased ([Arduino Forum: servo library documentation](https://forum.arduino.cc/t/servo-library-documentation/392773)).

“Left” usually means counter-clockwise in beginner servo documentation, but it is only meaningful if the viewing direction is defined ([MakeCode Adafruit servoWrite](https://makecode.adafruit.com/reference/pins/servo-write), [Arduino Servo.writeMicroseconds reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-writemicroseconds)).  For a small servo such as the SG90, “viewed from the output shaft/horn side” is the most teachable reference because students can look directly at the spline or horn and use ordinary clock-face language ([Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf), [MakeCode Adafruit servoWrite](https://makecode.adafruit.com/reference/pins/servo-write)).  Any worksheet or code comment that says “left,” “right,” “CW,” or “CCW” without a viewing reference can be physically reversed by flipping the servo over or by mounting the horn on the opposite side of a linkage ([Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).

## 3. Angular range convention

Software and education contexts most often describe a positional hobby servo with an absolute command scale from 0 to 180 degrees, with 90 degrees as the midpoint ([Arduino Servo.write reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-write), [Arduino Servo Motor Basics](https://docs.arduino.cc/learn/electronics/servo-motors/), [WPILib servo documentation](https://docs.wpilib.org/en/stable/docs/software/hardware-apis/motors/servos.html)).  Arduino’s beginner “Knob” and “Sweep” examples map values to 0-180 and sweep an RC servo shaft back and forth across 180 degrees ([Arduino Servo Motor Basics](https://docs.arduino.cc/learn/electronics/servo-motors/)).  WPILib likewise provides a scaled 0.0-1.0 setting and a `setAngle()` method using degrees from 0 to 180 for compatible servos ([WPILib servo documentation](https://docs.wpilib.org/en/stable/docs/software/hardware-apis/motors/servos.html), [WPILib Java Servo API](https://github.wpilib.org/allwpilib/docs/release/java/edu/wpi/first/wpilibj/Servo.html)).

Mechanical and RC descriptions often describe travel relative to neutral, such as approximately ±60 degrees for a Hitec default travel or approximately 90 degrees in each direction for the SG90 PDF’s 180-degree statement ([Hitec HS-5645MG spec sheet](https://www.hiteccs.com/public/uploads/data_sheet/HCS_HS-5645MG_Specsheetv2.2_-1729886570.pdf), [Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf)).  The relative convention is mechanically clearer when discussing linkages, trim, and end-point adjustment, because it names offsets from neutral rather than an arbitrary software zero ([Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf), [Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).  The best written convention is to include both forms, such as “command 90 degrees / 1500 µs / mechanical neutral” and “command 0 degrees / about 1000 µs / approximately -90 degrees from center if calibrated that way” ([Arduino Servo.write reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-write), [Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf)).

## 4. Recommended convention for STEM education up to Grade 12

The recommended classroom convention is to program with a 0-180 degree command scale because Arduino, MakeCode, Adafruit, and WPILib all present servo position in that student-friendly form ([Arduino Servo.write reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-write), [MakeCode Adafruit servoWrite](https://makecode.adafruit.com/reference/pins/servo-write), [WPILib servo documentation](https://docs.wpilib.org/en/stable/docs/software/hardware-apis/motors/servos.html)).  The recommended underlying signal explanation is that the servo receives one command pulse about every 20 ms, with 1500 µs as center and 1000-2000 µs as the conservative safe classroom range unless the datasheet and testing justify a wider range ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs), [SparkFun Hobby Servo Tutorial](https://learn.sparkfun.com/tutorials/hobby-servo-tutorial/all), [Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf)).  The recommended SG90-specific caveat is that many SG90 datasheets and Arduino defaults allow a wider endpoint range near 500-2400 µs or 544-2400 µs, but students should expand endpoints only after calibration and only while avoiding buzzing, growling, or hard mechanical stops ([Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf), [Arduino Servo library source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arduino-libraries/Servo/master/src/Servo.h), [Arduino Servo.writeMicroseconds reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-writemicroseconds)).

### Classroom specification to use

| Student command | Conservative pulse | SG90 extended / calibrated pulse | Relative position wording | Direction wording, if using the common Arduino convention |
|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| 0° | about 1000 µs | about 500-544 µs if calibrated safe | one end, about -90° from center on a 180° servo | left / CCW when viewed from the output shaft side, if that particular servo follows the convention ([Arduino Servo.writeMicroseconds reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-writemicroseconds), [Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf)). |
| 90° | about 1500 µs | about 1500 µs | center / neutral | midpoint; horn should be installed here before linkage adjustment ([ServoCity Servo FAQs](https://www.servocity.com/servo-faqs), [Arduino Servo.write reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-write)). |
| 180° | about 2000 µs | about 2400 µs if calibrated safe | other end, about +90° from center on a 180° servo | right / CW when viewed from the output shaft side, if that particular servo follows the convention ([Arduino Servo.writeMicroseconds reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-writemicroseconds), [Datasheet4U SG90 PDF](https://datasheet4u.com/pdf/791970/SG90.pdf)). |

Use 50 Hz / 20 ms as the default refresh rate unless a specific servo datasheet or controller library says otherwise ([Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail), [Hitec HFP-10 manual](https://www.cyber-hobbies.com/imatges/Servos/HFP-10.pdf)).  Define the viewing reference as “look straight at the servo horn or output spline from the shaft side” before using CW, CCW, left, or right ([MakeCode Adafruit servoWrite](https://makecode.adafruit.com/reference/pins/servo-write), [Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).  Teach that the pulse-width-to-angle mapping is a useful linear command model, not a precision measurement of the actual shaft angle, because Arduino maps linearly but Pololu warns that actual correspondence varies between servos ([Arduino Servo AVR implementation](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/arduino-libraries/Servo/master/src/avr/Servo.cpp), [Pololu servo-control article](https://www.pololu.com/blog/17/servo-control-interface-in-detail)).  Have students start at 90 degrees / 1500 µs, attach the horn mechanically at neutral, test 0 and 180 slowly, and reduce the endpoint range if the servo buzzes, growls, stalls, or hits a hard stop ([Arduino Servo.writeMicroseconds reference mirror](https://arduinogetstarted.com/reference/library/servo-writemicroseconds), [Arduino Forum: SG90 PWM settings](https://forum.arduino.cc/t/sg90-servo-pwm-settings/549644)).
